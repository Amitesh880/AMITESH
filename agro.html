Agroforestry

Definition

Agroforestry is the intentional integration of trees and shrubs into crop and animal farming systems to create environmental, economic, and social benefits. It combines agricultural and forestry technologies to create more diverse, productive, profitable, healthy, and sustainable land-use systems.

Key Practices

  • Alley Cropping: Planting crops between rows of trees to provide income while the trees mature.
  • Forest Farming: Cultivating high-value crops under the protection of a managed forest canopy.
  • Riparian Forest Buffers: Establishing tree, shrub, and grass plantings along waterways to filter farm runoff and stabilize banks.
  • Silvopasture: Combining trees with forage and livestock production.
  • Windbreaks: Planting trees and shrubs to protect crops, animals, and soil from wind.

Benefits

  • Improves soil health and reduces erosion.
  • Enhances biodiversity and provides wildlife habitat.
  • Increases farm productivity and profitability.
  • Sequesters carbon and mitigates climate change.
  • Improves water quality and reduces runoff.

Global Impacts

  • Contributes significantly to climate change mitigation by sequestering carbon.
  • Enhances food security and livelihoods in developing countries.
  • Reduces deforestation and promotes sustainable land use.
  • Supports biodiversity conservation and ecosystem services.
  • Improves resilience to climate extremes and variability.

Where They Are Practiced

  • Widely practiced in tropical regions, especially in sub-Saharan Africa.
  • Adopted in temperate regions like the USA and Europe for its multiple benefits.
  • Common in smallholder farms and subsistence agriculture.
  • Implemented in various forms such as alley cropping, silvopasture, and forest farming.
  • Used in both rural and urban settings to enhance sustainability.
