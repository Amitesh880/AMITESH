System of Rice Intensification

Definition

The System of Rice Intensification (SRI) is a farming methodology that aims to increase the yield of rice while using fewer resources and reducing environmental impacts. It focuses on changing the management of plants, soil, water, and nutrients to create a more productive and sustainable system of rice cultivation.

Key Practices

  • Transplanting Young Seedlings: Transplanting seedlings at a young age (8-12 days) to reduce transplant shock and promote vigorous growth.
  • Wider Spacing: Planting seedlings with wider spacing (25x25 cm or more) to reduce competition and promote better root and canopy growth.
  • Soil Aeration: Regularly aerating the soil to improve root growth and soil health.
  • Water Management: Keeping the soil moist but not continuously flooded to encourage stronger root systems.
  • Organic Fertilization: Using organic matter to enhance soil fertility and structure.

Benefits

  • Increases rice yields significantly compared to traditional methods.
  • Reduces water usage by up to 50%.
  • Improves soil health and fertility.
  • Reduces the need for chemical fertilizers and pesticides.
  • Enhances resilience to climate change and extreme weather conditions.

Global Impacts

  • Contributes to global food security by increasing rice production.
  • Promotes sustainable agriculture and resource use.
  • Reduces greenhouse gas emissions from rice paddies.
  • Supports smallholder farmers by increasing productivity and profitability.
  • Improves the livelihoods of farming communities.

Where They Are Practiced

  • Widely practiced in countries like India, China, Indonesia, and Vietnam.
  • Adopted in various regions of Africa and Latin America.
  • Implemented in both smallholder and large-scale rice farming systems.
  • Supported by various agricultural development programs and NGOs.
  • Increasingly recognized and promoted by international agricultural organizations.
